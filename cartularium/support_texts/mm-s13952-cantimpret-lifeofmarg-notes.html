12 divini spiritus habitaculum habilitabatur ad sanctimonie puritatem. Thomas is presumably attempting to legitimise Margaret in the eyes of the hierarchy by emphasising from the outset her similarity to “real” nuns. Note also his use of the word habitaculum: a little dwelling, thus underlining the fact that her cloister was a spiritual cloister and that she, as much as the cloistered nuns, was “alone with God.” Later in the life, he compares her heart to a chapel (para. 25). See Gerhard Bauer, Claustrum Animae: Untersuchungen zur Geschichte der Metapher als Kloster, Band I: Entstehungsgeschichte (München: Wilhelm Fink, 1973).

13 Compare Thomas's description of the young Lutgard: “But in a wondrous way, although she did not yet know the Lord (for He had not yet been revealed to her on any occasion), yet when she was alone, she sensed within her most inmost being I know not what of the divine. And, in fact, from that time on, the Lord was preparing from Himself within Lutgard a temple most worthy to be His dwelling place” (VLA I, 1; p. 5). Thomas always seems to use the word cognosco in the sense of direct knowledge.

14 levi motu animi affectum suum. The chapter title reads “How, when she once felt a stirring [of passion] (motum), she manfully ended it.”

15 incorporabat sibi.

16 dilectam hynnulam Christi tribuit libertati: a reference to Cant. 2:9; 2:17; 4:5; 7:3, 8:14. Thomas uses this metaphor for Lutgard in the same context and with the same conclusion: “the beloved fawn of Christ was permitted to rest in the sleep of contemplation. No one aroused her, no one kept her awake for pastoral cares unless she herself wished it, and it never happened that she so wished” (VLA II, 1; tr. p. 32).

17 inclusa: lit. “enclosed,” commonly used in reference to recluses.

18 Christina suffered from the same kind of embarrassment: “[After being ravished in the spirit and rolling and whirling around like a hoop and] when she was fully restored to herself, she knew what had happened from the tales of the others and she fled for shame and embarrassment and if anyone forcibly detained her, she languished with a great sorrow and thought herself stupid or foolish” (VCM 36; tr. p. 30). By the time Thomas wrote the VLA, his treatment of this kind of holy folly had become a little more subtle: [After showing her fingers which dripped with the oil of the Holy Spirit and dancing a mystic dance], she became drunk and was filled with exceeding and ineffable joy and with a spirit of folly” (VLA I, 16; p. 24). See John Saward, Perfect Fools: Folly for Christ's Sake in Catholic and Orthodox Spirituality (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1980).

19 See VMO 45: “One day she made plans to flee so that, unknown and despised among strangers, she might beg from door to door, that naked she might follow the naked Christ .... She frequently paid heed to and recollected Christ's poverty .... She burned with such a desire for poverty that she took with her a little bag in which to put alms and a little cup from which she might drink water or into which she might put food if it were given to her while she was begging” (tr. pp. 61–62). The term paupercula is a common epithet in these vitae for those women who went out to beg. Jacques calls Marie “a poor little woman” (VMO 45 and 46), Beatrijs of Nazareth is called Christi paupercula (I, 7, 37; p. 34) and Ida of Louvain is oddly labelled venerabilis Christi paupercula (7; p. 323). Thomas does not use the term for Christina— although she did, in fact, beg (VCM 22)—nor for Lutgard who was a nun, although it is used in VLA II, 33 in reference to a poor and needy woman.

20 In the same way Marie was forced to return because of the entreaties of her spiritual friends (VMO 45, tr. pp. 61–62).

21 cognitionis fruicione: lit. “fruition or full enjoyment of knowledge;” i.e. the Beatific Vision.

22 loca mire amentitatis et gracie: locus amoenus is the topos for Paradise.

23 This ability to distinguish feast days of individual saints is also found in the VMO: “She could distinguish one angel or saint from another in the same way that she could distinguish one of her neighbours from another .... When there was no announcement of a feast, she could distinguish in the secrecy of her heart between the festive days and those which were not festive, because solemn days were more sweet to the taste than ordinary days” (VMO 89; tr. p. 101–102).

26 hianti mentis ore: although this concept has an odd ring in English, it is a commonplace in mediæval mystical treatises. It carries with it the idea of the mind gasping with an almost sensuous desire for the waters of life.
