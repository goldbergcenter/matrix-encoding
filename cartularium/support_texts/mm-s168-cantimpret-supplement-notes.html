21 Giles was one of the founders of the monastery of Oignies. His career is outlined in the history of the priory which is translated below. According to that history, he was prior of the community from its inception in 1187 until his death in old age in 1233. The founding of a community could be a rather prolonged process. There is some disagreement about the precise moment when St. Nicholas of Oignies was founded.

22 Jacques de Vitry was born between 1160 and 1170, probably at Rheims. He studied at Paris and then, drawn by the fame of Marie d'Oignies, he became a canon regular in the monastery of St. Nicholas d'Oignies. In 1213 he preached against the Albigensians and later preached the fifth crusade. Humbert of Romans (+1277) said that Jacques' preaching moved the people of France in an unprecedented way. Jacques was consecrated bishop of St.-Jean-d'Acre, the port city of the Holy Land, on 31 July 1216. He participated in the crusaders' campaign in Egypt, 1218-1221; most of his surviving letters concern this campaign. He visited Europe in 1225 and in 1228 he resigned his see. In 1229 he was made cardinal-bishop of Tusculanum (Frascati). He died in May 1240. Thus R.B.C. Huygens, Lettres de Jacques de Vitry (Leiden: Brill, 1960): pp. 1-2, summarising Philippe Funk, Jakob von Vitry: Leben und Werken (Leipzig: Teubner, 1909): pp. 4-68. See Berlière, pp. 451-452.

23 Marie was born about 1177 into a wealthy and prominent family and was married at fourteen. Like many of her contemporaries who shared the same social and economic privileges, Marie and her husband abandoned their comfortable life for the vita apostolica, a life which tried to imitate the life of the early church of Jerusalem as presented in Acts 2-4. They decided to live in continence while serving the poor in a leper colony at Willambrouk. Marie's fame attracted so many visitors that, to find greater peace, she moved in 1207 to the priory of St. Nicholas of Oignies on the river Sambre about sixty kilometers south-southeast of Brussels not far from Namur. See Brenda M. Bolton, "Vitae Matrum: A Further Aspect of the Frauenfrage" in Medieval Women, ed. Derek Baker, Studies in Church History, Subsidia 1 (Oxford: Basil Blackwell, 1978): pp. 256-25.
