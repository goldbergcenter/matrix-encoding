Grammatica Mystica:

A Study of Bede's

Grammatical Curriculum1

Margot H. King

Toronto Ontario

Although valuable work has been done on the technique of Bede's grammatical textbooks2 and on his influence on the Carolingian schools,3 little material is available on the role which his De Arte Metrica et De Schematibus et Tropis played within the framework of monastic spirituality. In other words, what work has been done has been primarily devoted to a study of Bede's sources and to the influence which these textbooks exerted in the centuries following his death.

Grammar, to those twentieth century students fortunate enough to have studied it, usually brings to mind dry-as-dust rules about parts of speech and such abstruse topics as cases and voices. For a student in ancient Rome, however, it meant far more than this. It was the foundation upon which was built an entire educational system  and was, in fact, the first step in a carefully graduated ladder which progressed from what was called the trivium (grammar, rhetoric, dialectic) to the quadrivium (arithmetic, music, geometry and astronomy). Its focus was the attainment of a basic knowledge of the Latin language through a careful study of the “authorities” - that is to say, those classical authors whose works had become part and parcel of the cultural background - from which the student would acquire a solid grounding in the vital springs of Roman culture.

In Rome as in Greece, however, the mark of the truly educated person was not whether he had studied grammar but whether he had received training in rhetoric, an art which had been developed and perfected by the Greeks4 and which, says Marrou, “gave the Ancients a system of formal values that supplied prose with its own aesthetic” (p. 280). It is not surprising that such an attitude was anathema to the educated Christian of the early Church for whom Holy Scripture was the only authority, the only Book. As Jean Leclercq has shown, the role of “literature” in the early mediæval monastic context was completely spiritual in orientation:5 the written word took primacy over the spoken word and rhetoric began to be viewed with more and more suspicion.

In the eighth century, while the continent of Europe writhed in the death throes of Romanitas, a small corner of a backward nation saw the flowering of a genuinely Christian form of education which, according to one scholar, harmonised Scriptural comprehension, instruction in Christian literature, and fluency in Latin composition.6 It was thanks to the travels of the Northumbrian nobleman Benedict Biscop that the monasteries of Wearmouth and Jarrow became great centres of monastic learning during the seventh and eighth centuries. Here it was that the Venerable Bede, the “candle of the Church” as Boniface called him, prayed and worked and taught, and who, above all, provided the curriculum for the schools of Pepin and Charlemagne.7

Although we have few details concerning Bede's life, we know that within his monastery his life was quiet and followed a regular pattern of prayer, study, teaching, and writing. All his energies, he says, were devoted “entirely to the study of Scripture; and, amid the observance
observance of the discipline of the Rule and the daily task of singing in the Church, it has always been my delight to learn or to teach or to write.”8 Scriptural study, therefore, was the centre around which all his duties revolved. Intellectual endeavour was never undertaken for its own sake but always had Scriptural understanding as its goal.

Given this Scriptural foundation of the monastic vocation, it is obvious that monastic grammar must have been different from the grammar of either the classical grammarians or that of the later speculative grammarians. It approached its subject matter from a totally different perspective thus altering both its philosophy and its orientation. No longer a propaedeutic to the higher disciplines of rhetoric and dialectic, it was part of an educational curriculum which ignored the classical hierarchy of the trivium. The curriculum outlined by Charlemagne in his Admonitio9 sums up the direction taken by monastic education: letters were to be learned “so that you may the more easily and correctly penetrate the mysteries of Divine Scripture.”10 Practical and concrete in application, it had only one end: to enable the monk to achieve salvation through scriptural understanding.

The entire educational curriculum of the early mediæval monasteries mirrored the monastic vocation itself. Just as the postulant had to turn from the things of this world through what Cassian had called conversio,11 so too the novice had to turn his body and spirit and all his knowledge away from “the simulated and superstitious imaginings” of the pagan poets (DDC 2, 40, 60) towards the things of God from whom “everything is derived” (DDC 2, 38, 56). Practica, Cassian's next step - the active struggle of renunciation and the practice of virtue - is reflected in “practical” instruction in Latin, the language of Scripture and the liturgy. The final stage of theoria (contemplation) finds its grammatical counterpart in an assimilation of the scriptural mode of speech and thought so total that the divine truths expressed therein become an integral part of the reader. In this way a progressive doctrine of spiritual perfection replaced the progressive concept of the trivium and quadrivium of the ancient schools, a dynamic which was reflected in the grammatical curriculum itself.

The first duty of the novice was to learn to participate in the Daily Office, the “primary community service”12 around which the entire monastic life revolved. To this end, a young Anglo-Saxon entering Wearmouth or Jarrow had to learn Latin, a language which clearly bore no resemblance to his native tongue.13 That a knowledge of this new language was imperative is obvious when we consider that it was the vehicle for public worship in the western Church and, even more important, the language of the Vulgate. Furthermore, the formulation of Christian doctrine for the west had developed within a Roman context and, therefore, could only be expressed in Latin. As Dom Leclercq has pointed out “to think Christian doctrine otherwise than in Latin involved the danger of introducing into it inexactitudes or even errors.”14 While a deficiency in Latin could be partially remedied by means of translation for the idiotae (those who knew only one language), its lack precluded active participation in both the liturgical and intellectual life of the Church.

All those elements which pertained to the teaching of Latin were grouped under the term grammatica. The early Christian grammarians had echoed their classical masters in the definition of grammar. Cassiodorus, the exemplar of the Christian classicist, defined it in classical terms:

Grammar is skill in beautiful speech acquired from famous poets and writers. Its function is to create prose and verse without error. Its end is to please through skill in cultivated speech and faultless writing.15

Two centuries later, Alcuin, Bede's spiritual great-grandson,16 defined grammar in quite different terms:

Grammar is the knowledge of reading and is the guardian of right speaking and writing which is in accord with nature, reason, authority, and usage.17

Here the emphasis is on the written, not the spoken, word and skill and pleasure are not mentioned. We have moved from a world where rhetoric ruled supreme to one where the discovery of truth through an examination of the written word took precedence over the eloquence of the ancients.18

Although some scholars have professed bewilderment over the precise meaning of Alcuin's definition,19 the problem is clarified when one reads it in the monastic context of Augustinian theology.20 While the terms natura, ratio, auctoritas, and consuetudo had a technical meaning for the specialised classical grammarians,21 they took on a different meaning when grammar became the tool of Scriptural understanding. As Augustine makes clear in his De Doctrina Christiana, one can no longer separate grammar and theology once one has accepted the Christian mandate, and Augustine was the main inspiration for monastic education.22 It was through Bede and Alcuin that this tradition was passed on to the Carolingian educational system of the ninth century which, despite the existence of the Palace School and the emerging cathedral schools, was still predominantly monastic.23

If we admit, therefore, an educational tradition which goes back to Augustine, we can attempt to define Alcuin's definition. Grammar is the ability to read Scripture which, when understood, is the guardian which will ensure that what is spoken and written is grammatically correct and theologically sound. Furthermore, grammar must be in accord with nature, reason, authority, and usage. Nature is that divinely appointed order which arranges “things equal and unequal in a pattern which assigns to each its proper position,”24 while reason is that mental faculty which apprehends this order and ensures that it is followed in the movements of the human soul.25 Thus when nature and reason operate as God intended them to operate, the Christian recognises and obeys the authority of the expression of the Divine Law which, because it is divine, gives its usage the ultimate sanction.

Thus had grammar become the handmaid of monastic spirituality. It was the means whereby the monk could read and understand Holy Scripture, the supreme guide for all human activity, the arbiter of natural impulses and the director of reason. In a very real sense, it was the first step towards theoria for, only through the reading and understanding of Scripture, could one hope to begin to meditate and, finally, reach the heights of contemplation.

As a dedicated educator, Bede would have agreed with his spiritual father Augustine that the teacher of grammar was a person who, as “the expositor and teacher of Divine Scripture, the defender of right faith and the enemy of error, should both teach the good and extirpate the evil.”26 Rhetoric is to be eschewed because it seeks only to persuade and not to lead men to Truth.27 Grammar, on the other hand, consists of the exercise of those faculties whereby the truth contained in Scripture can be understood. The pleasure attendant upon eloquence and beauty which had been the goal of the ancient rhetorician is of only minor importance when compared with the ineffable truth contained in Holy Writ. Although aesthetic beauty can be found in Scripture, this beauty is the natural correlative of that Truth “which poured forth from the divine mind both wisely and eloquently.”28

Given this spiritual orientation, it follows naturally that the monastic grammatical curriculum reflected a similar orientation. The novice first memorised the Psalms, the basis of the Daily Office, and then learned the basic rules of Latin grammar and vocabulary through textbooks such as Donatus's De Partibus Orationis Ars Minor29 and Bede's own De Orthographia.30 At the same time, he was taught to chant the Psalms in a correct and seemly manner since the cantus was part of the grammatical curriculum as well as an introduction to the computus.31 Although the conjunction of grammar and the chant may seem curious to us, both have their basis in number which, according to Augustine, unites all things in the universe.32 Modulation, the basis of music,33 is to be found in the verbal arts as well as the mathematical sciences.34 As well, it should be the governing principle of every human act: all action should reflect the cosmic order of “the poem of the universe” which, in turn, obeys the “the laws of equality, unity and order.”35 Once again the monastic instinct reduces abstract theory to the practical end of salvation.

Although no textbooks of the cantus remain, Bede tells us that such manuals did exist: after his visit to Wearmouth, John, archchanter of the Church of St. Peter and abbot of the monastery of St. Martin in Rome, “left behind his numerous writings which are still preserved in the library [of Wearmouth] in which he set down what he had taught the monks concerning the order and manner of singing and reading the Psalms aloud (ordinem cantandi psallendi).36 Since the Psalms were considered to be metrical in form,37 a knowledge of rhythm and metre was essential to the cantus and music itself, according to Augustine, concerned rhythmus (proportion) and metrum (measure).38 Rhythm was learned by memorisation and  by what Bede called “the judgement of the ear” (iudicium auris).39 Metrics were learned by rote from the grammatical rules. As well as rhythm and metre, the student had to learn the proper intervals (positurae) between syllables and the accent proper to each syllable (prosodia). The study of intervals was important for correct pronunciation since vacans tempus influenced the pronunciation of the preceding syllable.40 With the decline of Latin as the common tongue, syllables had become roughly equal in duration and were no longer measured by the quantity of their vowels.41 Accent therefore became increasingly important for correct pronunciation. It would appear therefore that positura dealt with pronunciation of a phrase and required oral instruction, while prosodia treated the pronunciation of a word and utilised grammatical treatises for metrics and accentuation.

Such a grammatical treatise is Bede's De Arte Metrica. Despite its title, On the Metrical Art, it is clear that the aim of this textbook is practical instruction in pronunciation and not in metrics, since fully two-thirds of the treatise deals with quantity and accentuation. Metres are discussed only briefly: a definition of each, followed by examples primarily drawn from Christian authorities. The work ends with a short discussion of literary genres.

For metrics to have been dismissed like this would have been unthinkable to the ancient grammarians: for them the spoken word took precedence over the written word.42 In the monastic milieu, however, the written word ruled supreme and understanding of Scripture seen to be the basis of all human endeavour. No longer part of the rhetorical discipline, a study of metre was now only applied towards understanding a text which, since the time of Jerome, had been considered metrical in form.43 Thus Bede:

In the same fashion that I have tried to educate you in sacred letters and Church canons, I would in this way instruct you in the metrical art which is not unknown in the Divine Books (DAM 1, 25).

This view of the metrical character of Scripture goes back, of course, to the attempts of the early Church Fathers to counter charges that the Bible was filled with grammatical barbarisms. For them, the aesthetic beauty of pagan literature could in no way outshine the beauty of those Books which reflect the Divine Will. Just as music was seen to be the expression of the Divine Harmony, the truth found in Holy Scripture was an expression of the Divine Order. Since, for centuries, the metrical art had been viewed as the most perfect expression of human thought, it was inevitable that similar metrical devices must be found in those Books which reflect the Mind of God.

It is for this reason, therefore, that the examples Bede uses in his DAM are, for the most part, drawn from Christian authorities. The only pagan writer he quotes to any extent is Virgil,44 while Horace, Lucan and Lucretius are used only once. Unlike his spiritual masters, Bede was never tempted by the allure of pagan literature45 for, as Laistner has remarked, it is unlikely that he had read the pagan writers to any significant degree.46

Having now learned to participate in the Daily Office with correct pronunciation and accent and to read the words of Scripture with some degree of comprehension, the novice was now prepared to enter into a more formalised study of the Divine Books. He had moved from definition to the pronunciation of individual words and phrases and was now ready to pursue a study of units of thought, expressed through figures of speech.

This next step in the grammatical ladder is found in Bede's De Schematibus et Tropis, the companion-piece to the De Arte Metrica. This work is not, modern critics notwithstanding, a rhetorical treatise47 but an introduction to exegesis by means of a study of the literary devices used in Scripture. Through exegesis, the truth of Scripture could be so profoundly understood that it would be assimilated into the very being of the monk who would then, literally, live its truth and like Cædmon, quasi mundum animal ruminando,48 attain to union with it.

In the De Schematibus et Tropis Bede uses only examples from Scripture, with the exception of one quotation from the Christian poet Sedulius. In the De Arte Metrica he was able to afford to be freer in his use of non-scriptural examples since his aim was practical instruction in pronunciation. In the De Schematibus et Tropis, however, his goal is now entirely spiritual:

Holy Scripture is pre-eminent over all writings, not only by reason of its authority because it is divine, and by its utility because it leads to eternal life, but also by its antiquity and style (DST 2, 1, 11-16).

Thus Holy Scripture far surpasses pagan writings on both the supernatural and natural levels. As a direct reflection of the Divine Will it partakes of Truth in a way impossible to the pagans. Even when judged by pagan standards, there is no contest since its antiquity and style (beauty) both have their source in God the Creator.

Both Jerome and Augustine had held that instruction in the methods required for an understanding of the literary devices found in Scripture was essential in order to benefit most fully from its teachings.49  Such understanding can only be accomplished if the reader can penetrate through the stylistic devices to the truth contained therein. By the proper use of what Augustine had called the “lower reason,” one understands and submits to the truth of Scripture and thereby moves into the sphere of the “higher reason.”50 In this way, knowledge (scientia), “the rational cognizance of temporal things,” leads to wisdom (sapientia), “the intellectual cognizance of eternal things,”51 “the seventh and last step of the spiritual life which [the holy person] enjoys peace and tranquillity ... [and there] devote himself to the contemplation of things Divine and the knowledge of most sacred thoughts.”52 As Gilson has said:

Once our science has been transformed by faith, it is subject to the workings of wisdom and benefits from the help of grace; what was once but vain curiosity lacking both beginning and end becomes now a legitimate desire to understand revealed truth .... The saving truth to which we adhere by faith is made known to us through the Scriptures. we must therefore be able to read and interpret them .... [This is] the ideal of Christian wisdom gathering the various sciences unto itself, and the ideal of a system of human knowledge organised under the hegemony of the Scriptures which are themselves depositories of the saving truth revealed to men by God.53

Clearly Augustine's wisdom is identical to what Cassian had called theoria and it is this which Bede sees as the ultimate goal of Scriptural interpretation. The spiritual purification and purgation which accompany meditation upon the Scriptures are the means whereby, with infused grace, one can attain to a mystical foretaste of the joys of Paradise. Cassian divided theoria into two parts, the historical and the spiritual understanding of Holy Scripture, the latter being further divided into the familiar three levels of meaning: the tropological, allegorical and anagogical.54 Hence it is that through an understanding of the “literary” devices of Scripture, one can ascend to the ultimate goal of wisdom.

In his De Schematibus et Tropis, Bede follows his masters and examines the style of Scripture in its use of seventeen schemes (changes in the order of words) and thirteen tropes (changes in the meaning of words).  He includes the four-fold method of Scriptural interpretation as a subspecies of astismos under the general species of allegory.55 It must be emphasised, however, that in practice neither Bede nor his predecessors rigidly adhered to this method of exegesis56 and, as Professor Jones has persuasively argued, such an approach to Scripture was “intended to free the faithful readers from inherited claims of reason by attributing cogency to shifting points of view.”57 Rather than a scheme by which God's creation is forced into a rigid “allegorical” framework, allegory itself is an indirect testimony to human temporality. Since men and women exist in time and the Oneness of God is beyond time, human understanding of this Unity can be, at the best, only partial and incomplete. The four-fold method of exegesis, therefore, serves the purpose of being able to make the reader see this Unity from different angles, depending on the circumstances or the causes.58

Bede's definition of allegory affirms the flexibility of this system of scriptural interpretation. Allegory, he says, is only one of the thirteen tropes. Further, there are seven species of allegory which can concern either facts or words, and each of these fourteen kinds can have anywhere from one to four senses. For Bede, as for his predecessors, scriptural exegesis was the application of a spiritual commonsense to shifting aspects of the unchanging Word of God.

It is clear, therefore, that Bede's grammatical textbooks have a much different purpose from those of the earlier grammarians. Purely vocational in purpose, the sole end of grammar was to understand Scripture so profoundly that its truth would become the means of sustaining the monk's spiritual life. From simple pronunciation to Scriptural exegesis, Bede's emphasis is always on the use to which these abilities will be put. At each level of instruction, novices were taught whatever might be of use to them as aspirants to wisdom, whether it be in the execution of the Daily Office, in the Chant, or in the reading of God's Truth as contained in Holy Scripture.

Bede's own life remains a testimony to the efficacy of the monastic educational system. In his person we find - as, some twelve centuries later, we find in Charles Jones, his spiritual son - the inspired teacher, the Latin-trained exegete, the poet and the preacher. From his own meditation upon Scripture, he passed on to others the spiritual fruits he had obtained so that he and they might find those joys which accompany wisdom and contemplation. Thus he ended his life in teaching and chanting the Psalms and “the whole of every night he passed cheerfully in prayer and giving God thanks.”59 The Ecclesiastical History finishes with a prayer which sums up the ideals by which Bede lived and which are to be found, in embryo, in the little grammatical textbooks he composed for his students:

And I pray thee, merciful Jesus, that as Thou has graciously granted me sweet draughts from the Word which tells of Thee, so wilt Thou, of Thy goodness, grant that I may come at length to Thee, the fount of all wisdom and stand before Thy face for ever (HE 5, 24).
