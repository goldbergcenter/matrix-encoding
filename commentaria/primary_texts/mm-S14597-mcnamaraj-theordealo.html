The Ordeal of Community: Hagiography & Discipline in Merovingian Convents1

Jo Ann McNamara

Few people have ever attempted a social experiment more radical than the ascetic communalism of early mediaeval times. The vision that inspired them was intrinsically contradictory. Asceticism was a highly individualistic experience aimed at the perfection of the inner self. It was often described as alienatio, rejection of the social responsibilities inherent in community life. The consecrated woman’s most radical and outstanding characteristic was her commitment to perpetual celibacy which enhanced the tendency to self-absorption inherent in her religious vocation. The celibate was free of the competing affections and loyalties which detract from a married person’s total commitment to an external community. But she was also free of the familial authority which ordinarily socialised people as components of a larger community. This was an experience generally unknown to women of antiquity whose identity was entirely shaped in conformity to family roles. During the first three Christian centuries, women created new forms of self-realisation within the context of virginity and chaste widowhood.2

The early ascetics tended to invent their way of life as they went along. When Jerome visited Rome for the first time, he encountered a circle of aristocratic women who met regularly in study groups to explore the possibilities of the new way of life. European women migrated to Egypt and Asia Minor to study and emulate the cenobitic communities there. In fourth and fifth century Gaul, asceticism appealed to isolated individuals or small groups. Often a continent married couple formed the core of an ascetic household. Some individuals sought the seclusion of a hermitage while others lived alone in cities, like Saint Genovefa in Paris. Still others shared their homes with like-minded companions.3 Nevertheless, the character of a uniform movement was preserved by voluminous exchanges of letters, biographies and exemplary tales in a wide network. The celibate women of the west knew of Augustine’s informal letter of guidance to some of his female friends, which moderns have called the Rule for Nuns.4

Pressures on their independence came from the outside, from the protective authority of the male clergy and from the destructive violence of the male predator.As the barbarian invasions disrupted life for everyone in Gaul, religious people began to seek shelter in places like the island of Lérins off the coast at Cannes where a small monastic community of celibate and married people vowed to abstinence was established.5 Alternatively, they gathered in confined establishments like Saint Victor of Marseille founded by John Cassian. There the ordeal of community began. The communal form of asceticism, which emerged in the west during the period of the invasions, required that the personal strength and devotion that led women and men to reject the secular world be ruthlessly suppressed or channelled into more altruistic expressions. The proud and the adventurous who perceived themselves to be God’s chosen vessels, brides of Christ, had to discipline themselves to humility and obedience. They had to deny the instincts of ownership even over objects of personal use and they had to school themselves to long silences designed not only to promote meditation but to avert the impulses to gossiping, backbiting and pontificating which generally irritate any social group.

The success of a monastic community depended on the capacity of its director to mold its individual members into a harmonious sisterhood. By the end of the fifth century, it was clear that the spirit of charity was insufficient to control and maintain the communal experiment. A way of life that was individualistic in origin, whose attraction lay precisely in its rejection of communal authority and responsibility, did not easily bend to the conflicting needs of a group. Proximity obliged even prospective saints to formulate guidelines for a religious life that comprehended women and men, some married and some celibate but all rejecting the bonds of sexuality and familial authority. Cassian’s compilations of exemplary stories and wise sayings from the desert fathers of the east were produced for that purpose.6 These were followed by more formal rules.

Even though every monastic community was autonomous, supported by its own financial resources and governed by a superior elected by the community or appointed by the community’s patron, episcopal and secular authorities periodically intervened with legislation to prevent any community from deviating from standards of orthodoxy and church discipline. This laid the basis for the institutionalisation of the ascetic vision and communities began to regulate themselves more systematically. No women wrote the rules which were published and disseminated for their use in this period. Abbesses and foundresses did, however, study this mass of legislation and piece together the customs of their individual communities according to their own judgment. Little by little, female monasticism was moving from a self-generated alternative to family life toward an institutionalised alternative whose conditions were legislated by men, though women determined their application.

The first western rule was written by bishop Caesarius of Arles for a community of women under his sister’s leadership.7 The same rule was adapted for another community later in the sixth century by Bishop Aurelian of Arles. It was then brought into northern Gaul by Saint Radegund for the use of her community of Sainte Croix in Poitiers.8 Though we lack concrete evidence, there is every reason to suppose that it was widely read and adapted by many other female communities at this period. The Rule of Benedict of Nursia was written in the latter half of the sixth century.9 By 600, it was being actively promoted by Pope Gregory I for communities of both women and men. Though rarely adopted in its entirety, it was certainly known in Gaul at that time and many of its elements were in use in Gallic communities. Similarly, the Rule of Columbanus, introduced by the Irish monks who established Luxeuil, was widely consulted by Gallic monastic leaders.10 Two rules written expressly for communities of women by Bishop Donatus of Besançon and Abbot Waldebert of Luxeuil respectively indicate that most communities combined individual elements of all these rules as best suited their own needs.11

Merovingian monasteries cannot be easily sorted out constitutionally. There was as yet no distinction made between “orders” or even houses with an active or contemplative commitment. Each institution was autonomous in its governance and provided its own source of funds. Women and men faced roughly the same difficulties. The basic rules promulgated by Benedict and Columbanus were not gender specific and the more elaborate rules of Donatus and Waldebert, though addressed to specific female communities, do not impose any regulations that would not be just as suitable for men. Communities of both genders looked to their resident leaders for guidance. We can gain considerable insight into this process through the study of hagiography. The saint’s life as a literary genre burgeoned in Merovingian Gaul and at least eighteen spiritual biographies of women have survived from this period. All of them concern abbesses or foundresses of female communities and throw much light on the daily lives of the sisters.12

In some respects the ordeal of community was more stringent for women than for men. Even within religion, women did not enjoy the range of options open to men. The latter might spend some part of their lives in monasteries and the rest in a hermitage or a bishopric. The latter alternative was flatly closed to women and the former was apparently socially unrealistic. When Saint Waldetrude (alone among the female saints of the period) installed herself in a hermitage she was subjected to unrelenting pressure from her saintly sister Aldegund and others to use her resources more effectively for the support of a community. Moreover, while both female and male communities were subjected to the discipline of the bishops and councils of the church at large, none of these higher authorities were or could be women. Thus in the final analysis, the limits on the aspirations of the community and on the authority of the abbess were established in accordance with male judgments.

The effect of this constraint is difficult to assess. Consecrated women shared the goals of religious men and cooperated actively in carrying them out. On the other side, dissenters have not been commemorated in a literature which, though sometimes written by women, was subject to male approval for its publication. Painful contradictions developed, for example, in the matter of claustration.13 The goal of monasticism was withdrawal from the world. Neither female nor male ascetics were supposed to wander about unsupervised or shift restlessly from one monastery to another. However, the basic rules of Columbanus and Benedict which were adopted by communities of women as well as men did not repeat the command for strict claustration introduced into Caesarius of Arles’ rule for nuns. As long as each community remained free to devise its own house rules, abbesses could apply the rule as they saw fit and the evidence of hagiography indicates that they applied it pragmatically as they judged it would serve the needs of the moment. In writing a rule for his mother, Donatus of Besançon discarded claustration as unsuitable for women in a relatively isolated rural setting. But it is clear that women never travelled in and out as freely as their male brethren did. Men whose temperaments clashed with those of their fellow monks could take to the road and found new communities. Or they could work as wandering preachers. That experience is not paralleled in the lives of female saints. Sometimes a bishop might take a nun out of one community and send her to direct a new sisterhood. But, as the lives of Bertilla, Sadalberga and Austreberta demonstrate, these were women with proven records in the communal life, not dissidents or innovators.

On the other side, women did not have the capacity to seclude themselves as effectively as they may have wished to do. It is probable that there were establishments of men which effectively barred all women from the cloistered precincts. But there was no known community of nuns without some considerable detachment of men who came and went freely and their regulation was one of the problems confronting every abbess and her subordinate officials. Chaplains, working men, neighbourhood magnates and prelates, relatives, travellers, invalids and beggars appear in hagiographical records with no apologies or explanations from the authors. On trial before the assembled bishops at Poitiers, the rebellious nun Clothild accused the abbess Leubevera of keeping a lover disguised as a woman in the convent.14 She singled out a man who was indeed wearing female dress. He claimed that he did so to symbolise his impotence. Then Clothild accused the abbess of turning men into eunuchs. As it turned out, the man in question had been castrated by a doctor on the advice of the saintly foundress Radegund because his testicles were incurably diseased. The abbess was found innocent but obviously even in the day of the saintly and highly reclusive foundress the cloistered faith healer was in regular consultation with a male medical consultant and personally attended invalids even when the disease was so intimate as a testicular cancer.

This constant casual male presence posed obvious problems of discipline for the abbess but there were other highly unsuitable persons within the community itself. Many women, like the discontented Clothild, were hustled out of the world as soon as the monastery became an established institution in Gaul and they had a tendency to pursue their own ambitions at the risk of permanent disruption of the communal peace. Other women lived temporarily or permanently in monasteries though they had no evident vocation for the religious life. Others still were virtual prisoners in the convents. Theudechild, widow of King Charibert, had entrusted her fortune to his brother Guntram who pretended that he wished to marry her. He robbed her and confined her to a convent in Arles. When she rebelled against the fasts and vigils and attempted to elope with a Spaniard, the abbess beat her and locked her into a cell until she died.15 Similarly, Radegund enclosed a penitent who had escaped from the convent by a rope over the walls. Bishop Felix of Nantes used a convent as a prison for his niece who wanted to marry against his will. She contacted her lover who rescued her from the convent.16 King Charibert’s daughter Bertefled escaped from Ingitrude’s convent in Tours. The abbess was glad to see her leave and said she was a woman “who ate and slept a lot and had no interest at all in the holy office.”17

Sometimes, unconsecrated women sought the convent simply for protection. Gregory of Tours told many tales involving women in sanctuary. Though he never says exactly how they maintained their living arrangements, it is probable that they made use of conventual buildings joined physically to churches or within their precincts so that the nuns could attend religious services without going into the streets. Gregory noted that Ingitrude of Tours built her convent in the courtyard of Saint Martin’s church. This would have been a logical place to house the daughters of the adventurous nobleman Guntram Boso who was in the habit of depositing them in “the holy church at Tours” for safety.18 It was probably the convent attached to Saint Saturnin of Toulouse that sheltered the wife of Ragnovald who had already been robbed and forced out of a sanctuary in Agen.19 The same site harboured the princess Rigunth, daughter of Ragnovald’s enemy Chilperic, who had been robbed of her immense dowry while on her way to be married. Again, some conventual shelter must have been available when Rigunth’s mother, the strong-willed Queen Fredegund, took sanctuary in Paris while rallying her murdered husband’s allies to her cause.20

If indeed these women voluntarily sought sanctuary in convents attached to churches, they may have been willing to respect the rules and customs of the place while they enjoyed its hospitality. Even so, it is hard to see how the religious mission of the nuns could have been conducted in a routine manner with all the comings and goings of the fugitives’ relatives and friends; the business and gossip of the inevitable entourage who must have accompanied them; and the occasional violence of their pursuers. A woman named Caesaria with her brother-in-law took sanctuary and when she came too near the church door she was snatched out by her waiting enemies.21 Even nuns were dragged unwillingly from the convent. Bishop Syagrius of Autun was reproved by Gregory I for not preventing the marriage of a nun abducted from her cloister. The wicked Eulalius, who was already married to a woman named Tetradia, abducted a nun from her convent in Lyon after he had killed her cousin.22 The church also provided the avenue of attack for the enemies of saints Rusticula and Anstrude who tried to drag them from their convents to answer charges of meddling in secular politics.

Unlike later convents, the typical Merovingian institution housed women from every rung of the social ladder and most of the saints used their fortunes to provide shelter for the helpless, like the six female slaves ransomed by Bishop Bercharius in 696 who formed the core of his community at Puellemoutiers. Though women of servile origins were unlikely to be the leaders of any attempt at insubordination, there is no guarantee that they were really prepared to live like nuns. They may have been more interested in improving their social status, a motive recognised and strictly forbidden in the Rules of Donatus and of Waldebert. On the other side, Caesarius had to restrain the noble sisters of Arles from attempting to turn their humbler sisters into personal servants.

The first generation of monastic women in Gaul after the conversion of the Franks were often refugees from forced marriages.23 In other cases, the convent was used as a depository for unmarried or married women whose presence in the world proved inconvenient. Though clerical marriage remained a strong option for women with religious aspirations, conciliar legislation repeatedly aimed at limiting their activities and influence. In 541, the Council of Orleans ordered clerical couples to live in continence and to occupy separate chambers.24 Gregory of Tours described the indignation of the wife of Felix of Nantes against this prescription. Hoping to catch her husband with another woman or to seduce him back to the conjugal bed, she went into his chamber in the middle of the night. A vision of a gleaming lamb asleep on his chaste breast deterred her from her purpose.25 The Council of Tours, in 567, resorted to more heroic measures by trying to keep wives and their attendant female servants out of clerical households altogether.26 They were ordered to set up a separate establishment (almost a mini-convent) and enter the bishop’s house only to supervise the housekeeping. To increase the similarity between clerical wives and nuns, bishops’ wives were to receive a special habit and blessing at the time of their husband’s ordination. Hagiography confirms that at least some clerical couples did separate upon the elevation of the husband. The wife of bishop Faro of Meaux was so contented with her monastic retreat that she repulsed his efforts to bring her back to the married state.27 But other wives and widows of clergymen may well have entered convents against their wills, or at least against their better judgment. The same council prohibited the widows of the clergy from remarriage.28

Other women who were not married to priests and had no independent fortunes who were consecrated to virginity or chaste widowhood had customarily been taken into clerical households where they acted as servants. Not surprisingly, these instances of clerical cohabitation with women were no more popular than clerical marriages. A flood of legislation excluded such women from priestly houses.29 The point was illustrated by one of Gregory the Great’s exemplary tales. The saintly bishop of Fulda maintained a group of consecrated women in his house where they enjoyed his protection in exchange for household services. One night, a Jewish visitor hurried to his establishment with a warning that he had been vouchsafed the previous night in a vision. Caught on the road, the Jew had sought shelter in an abandoned pagan temple only to discover that it was a midnight trysting place for the local demons. An unwilling eavesdropper, he was shocked to hear them chortling with glee when one devil reported that he had come close to bringing the bishop to a fall. He had induced him to give his housekeeper an affectionate pat on the back and anticipated more familiar embraces soon. Without hesitation, the virtuous bishop sent the women to a monastery. For Gregory, the story ended happily with the conversion of the awe-stricken Jew.30 But if the women who were thus dispossessed of their home and livelihood objected to a convent, they had little recourse. The same councils prohibited consecrated virgins and widows from marrying, no matter what the circumstances. The second Council of Tours, specifically applied the law to women who claimed to have taken the veil to avoid forcible abduction and alluded to laws of Kings Childebert, Clothar and Charibert on the same subject.31

Despite this stringency, there was no restriction on the age at which a girl might enter a monastery. The lives of female saints include many references to infants and young girls harboured by nuns for a variety of reasons. Baudonivia claimed that Radegund had raised her from the cradle, just as Gertrude of Nivelles raised her niece and successor Wulftrude and Aldegund raised her sister’s daughter Aldetrude.32 Balthild brought her infant god-daughter into the convent with her.33 Other children appear in these accounts who were not related to the abbess or wealthy foundress: Radegund cured the infant sister who shared the cell of one of her nuns.34 Burgundofara was said to have raised a child at Faremoutiers in such perfect innocence that she was unable to distinguish women from men.35 Moreover, many of these children were not being raised to become nuns at all. One of the problems raised during an outbreak of discontent at Poitiers was that the abbess had married her niece out of the convent, dressed in a gown made of one of its best altar cloths. In other cases, intergenerational conflicts between parent and child might threaten to drag the whole community into strife. At her mother’s urging, Bertegund, daughter of Ingitrude of Tours, left both her husband and children. The bishop Gregory of Tours responded by going to the convent personally and reading her the decree of the Council of Gangres condemning women who claimed that heaven was closed to married women.36 Bertegund eventually brought her son into the convent with her and persuaded her brother, the bishop of Bordeaux, to annul her marriage. On behalf of her husband, King Guntram attacked the bishop and accused him of sleeping with her servants but he continued to protect his sister in her life as a nun. Still she had not achieved peace: she quarreled violently with her mother and, after much contention, left the convent. Ingitrude then excluded her daughter from her will and explicitly forbade her return to the convent at Tours after her death. In retaliation, Bertegund attacked the convent and stripped it of its furnishings.37

The most famous and well-documented instance of conventual strife in this period occurred in the foundation of Saint Radegund at Poitiers shortly after the deaths of the saint and the first abbess Agnes. Basina, Chilperic’s daughter with his first wife Audevera, had been forced into a convent when the ambitious slave Fredegund had replaced her mother in the royal bed.38 Some years later, Chilperic attempted to reclaim his daughter but she was apparently unwilling to accept his demand that she marry. On the basis of the conciliar decrees cited above, Radegund refused to release her back into the world.39 Though Basina appears to have elected the nun’s life, she later became discontent at the way it was conducted at Poitiers. She joined with Clothild, a natural daughter of King Charibert, in revolt against the incumbent abbess Leubevera. Forty or more nuns joined the rebellion and left the convent. Some of them accompanied Clothild to King Guntram’s court while the majority took sanctuary in Saint Hilary’s church. There they tried to maintain some sort of communal life until Gregory of Tours dispersed them because they were unable to provide themselves with fuel for the winter.40 Meanwhile some of the nuns got pregnant and some married. Others recruited a gang of thugs and attempted to break into their old convent and kidnap the abbess.

When the bishops finally gathered to hear the accusations of the rebel nuns, they were treated to a list of grievances that dramatically illustrates the dimensions of the communal problem. Leubevera was charged with providing poor food for her flock and allowing servants and workmen to use their bathroom. She was accused of playing backgammon, entertaining lay visitors and dressing her niece in the altar cloths. The abbess replied that their food was at least plentiful; that the bathroom sharing was a temporary expedient during repairs; that the rule did not forbid backgammon; and that she followed the custom of the foundress in giving banquets to Christian travellers but did not eat with them. She admitted that she had outfitted her niece and held an engagement party for her which the bishop himself attended.41 The abbess was acquitted of all charges. Basina returned to the monastery where she can hardly have been a force for peace during the remainder of her life.42

The revolt of the nuns at Poitiers is famous because of the celebrity of the bishop of Tours who presided over its resolution and included a lengthy account of the affair in his history of the Franks. It would be foolish to imagine that it was an isolated event. As long as convents were populated with a percentage of women who were rebellious when they entered or whose natures were not particularly suited to the demands of the life, we must expect their harmony to be disrupted.

Thus the abbesses of the sixth and seventh centuries faced a monumental task. They were still actively involved in charting a social wilderness, for the dimensions of the monastic life and its capacities were still largely unknown. They were free to formulate many of the specific conditions under which they would live but ultimately their freedom was limited by the human capabilities of their sisters on the one side and the authority of their male superiors on the other. They could not count on having communities of women who had willingly embraced the life they were leading or willingly chosen their officials, though the rules assume both conditions as basic to the success of the monastic life. When Saint Benedict encountered opposition from his reluctant monks (who attempted to poison him rather than live in austerity), he left them. When Saint Austreberta was similarly threatened by the nuns of her newly gathered community at Pavilly she had no alternative but to remain with them and trust to divine intervention to thwart their attempts to murder her or provoke the local count to do her violence.43

Increasingly, violence was also embedded in the rules. Seventh century rules, stemming from the inspiration of Columbanus, tend to be harsher and more detailed in prescribing punishment than sixth century rules. This probably reflects the growing experience of the monastic regulators. Maternal discipline did not spare the rod. Abbess Rictrude’s daughter Eusebia, who was determined to escape from her mother’s convent in order to take up her inherited responsibilities as abbess in her deceased grandmother’s community, was so severely beaten for her insubordination that she coughed blood for the rest of her life.44Such punishments must certainly have had the effect of subduing the most boisterous of the nuns. All the surviving rules of the seventh century make free use of physical punishment to achieve that end.

On the other side, however, the lives of foundresses and abbesses exemplify the need for stern discipline within the context of maternal responsibility. Even the redoubtable Rictrude argued that she applied the whips to her daughter only because she feared that the girl (who was still under age) would suffer worse things in the next world if she failed in her new community as a result of insufficient training in the old one. Radegund often said that her nuns were her chosen daughters, dearer than any natural children she might have had.45 The wailing of her nuns for their lost mother which Gregory of Tours noticed at her funeral complemented this sentiment.46 When a nun at Poitiers who had escaped over the walls returned repentant, Radegund had her immured for life. The nun was repaid for her consent to this imprisonment with ecstatic visions and ultimately an inspiring death.47

Within the community, the elaborate lists of crimes and punishments supplied by the rules were illustrated by the examples of saints and stories of divine intervention which the nuns read constantly or heard during their meals and recreational periods. For example, Jonas of Bobbio warned discontented nuns against escape with a hair-raising tale of rebellious nuns at Faremoutiers.A group of women chafing under the restrictions of their life tried to escape over the walls on ropes during the night. Most of them were prevented by divine intervention in the shape of a flaming thunderball which woke the sleeping community and blocked the exits until the women could be recaptured. Restored to the community, two of the women continued to resist making full confession and would not allow the abbess to guide them back into the sisterhood. Soon both lay dying of fatal fevers and the sisters who gathered at their beds with the abbess to pray for them, received a powerful lesson from their delirious ravings. Both women screamed that they were being attacked by a horde of Ethiopians and died begging to be released from their rapacious clutches. The sisters testified that long afterward cries and wailing issued from their tombs until the abbess was finally obliged to disinter them. Nothing remained of their bodies but ashes still warm from some dreadful supernatural fire.48

Donatus’ rule provided for the abbess to hear daily confessions from her nuns and give them the necessary penitential direction.49 Waldebert advised that a systematic review of faults be undertaken three times a day.50 In this manner, the nuns were encouraged to police themselves and to forestall punishment by correcting their faults before they could be expressed in detectible deeds. The spirit of anger, so inimical to communal harmony, was a constant threat to which the saintly abbesses themselves were sometimes subject. Rage and harshness of spirit so stifled Sadalberga that she once fell into a near fatal fever which deprived her of the power of speech. Later she told her anxious sisters that she had been given a vision of two winged beings who drove away the angry spirits with their swords, leaving her with a weakened body but a stronger mind.51 Abbess Bertilla was once obliged to call a sister back from the dead to because she had not yet obtained forgiveness for an angry outburst against her subordinate.52

Greed was another constant provocation, particularly for food and drink. Every rule restricts the monastic diet to a rational but austere measure. The diners must often have been encouraged to suppress their discontent by the reading of the lives of saints famous for their fasting and for their willingness to share their food with their sisters and the poor. Miracles were cited to show divine concern with the convent food supply.53 A most salutary punishment was visited upon a nun at Faremoutiers who was a compulsive eater habitually raiding the pantry when the other nuns were not watching. Finally she was afflicted with a strange food phobia which made her unable to eat anything but tree bark and dead leaves washed down with the dregs from the brewing. When the unfortunate sister sat down to this miserable fare, she found herself facing a phantasmic boar for her dinner companion who snuffled and grunted over his slops throughout the meal, a mirror held up to her vice.54

Nuns also suffered from a tendency to let worldly rank and status interfere with the equality of sisterhood. All their rules were firm in stating that the only acceptable distinctions within a community were those of virtue and office.Indeed, the offices themselves—the abbess and prioress elected by the community and their adjutants whom they appointed—were intended to be allotted also as a reward for virtue. The other sisters, as the rules generally delineate, were ranked according to seniority in religion.The sisters who had been longest in the convent took the lead in choir, at table and in all processional activity unless they were demoted for some lapse in discipline.The rules also emphasise the pre-eminence of humility as a monastic virtue: the high-born were to suppress their pride following the example of noble saints, and the low-born were to refrain from presuming upon their condescension. In life, Radegund forsook her queenship and its trappings and devoted herself to cleaning the shoes of her sisters and even to caring for the latrines. But she reached down from heaven to punish a servant who dared to sit in her former throne.55 Austreberta’s successor at Pavilly succumbed to the spirit of pride and allowed the community to deny admittance to a low-born postulant. Despairing for her vocation, the woman grabbed at the saint’s funeral pall and refused to release it until her application was reconsidered. The abbess scornfully rebuked her for attempting to use the convent and its patron saint to raise herself to a rank which her birth had denied her. From the grave, Austreberta struck the abbess with fever which would not leave her until she had “happily remembered what she had so unhappily forgotten: that in Christ there is neither bond nor free.”56

Pride in spiritual accomplishment was another danger. In Burgundofara’s community a nun named Domna had achieved such holiness that it took visible form as rays of light streaming from her mouth when she sang in choir.Before the careful abbess could stop them, two little girls in the community indiscreetly drew the community’s attention to the wonder. Unhappily, the news proved her undoing.She became puffed up by the favour and lost her former humility and when she came to die no choirs of angels attended her passing, as they did for her young admirers who were given the exemplary death once reserved for Domna.57

Laziness and disobedience were also prime enemies of community life. After her death, Austreberta was a relentless regulator of her remaining sisters.She sent a fever which nearly killed a lazy girl who was in the habit of taking noonday naps on the saint’s discarded bed.58 Another nun was punished by a hard blow from the saint who appeared in a vision at midnight policing the dormitory to ensure that every sister had joined the community at prayer on her feast day.59

But punishment and rigid enforcement of the rule could at best only achieve the discipline of a prison.In complement to the rules, the lives of saints, regularly read to the communities under their patronage, were designed to dwell on the positive effects of the regular discipline and the maternal character of the abbess. There we glimpse young women like Wulftrude of Nivelles, Baudonivia, Rusticula, Anstrude and Austreberta who entered the convent as children and were raised there by the nuns.They were guarded and educated, learning to read and sometimes encouraged to write as well. Through example and instruction the older sisters taught humility, obedience and sisterly love to the younger and they, in their turn, enforced those lessons as abbesses in later life. Burgundofara was called back from heaven itself to continue her earthly life because her young niece Gibetrude was not yet old enough to thrive without her aunt’s guidance. Deurchild, a young girl brought into Faremoutiers by her mother, prevailed to save her parent by a display of precocious perseverance when the older woman wearied of the austere life.60

Baudonivia, who was raised from infancy by Radegund, reported that the saint had God’s promise that she should shepherd her flock before her into Paradise and she looked back in later years when the convent was wracked with strife, wistfully remembering that Radegund had once raised her protecting hand and sheltered the cloister from the attack of thousands of demons.She had not even suffered a raucous night bird to threaten the peace and quiet of her cloister.61 Fortunatus thought that her humble labour was so appealing to God that He killed a mouse who had the temerity to try to bite the thread she was spinning before it could close its jaws.62 The community at Pavilly who had responded to Austreberta’s high standards by trying to get her killed or deposed were finally won over by her humility.The watchful prioress encountered her superior patrolling the convent by night to ensure that all was well. Unable to see her in the darkness, she ordered the abbess to stop prowling and go to the cross if she could not sleep. Austreberta did not reveal her identity but went obediently to the chapel where she passed the night praying before the crucifix until the somewhat embarrassed prioress gave her leave to go.63

Nuns were encouraged through their reading to believe that miracles would relieve their daily rounds of menial labour. Thus Aldetrude, Aldegund’s little niece who was being raised in her convent, accidentally set fire to a pot of candle wax which she was melting down for her aunt. However, her swift confidence that God would respect her obedience to her duties enabled her to plunge her bare hands into the fire and rescue the community property without any injury.64 Likewise, when Austreberta was taking her turn at baking the community’s bread, her inexperienced companion failed to clear the fire completely out of the heated oven and the bread caught fire. The sisterhood would have lost their food had not the saint intrepidly reached into the fire and pulled out the smoking loaves with her sleeves safe in her virtue from all injury.65 A nun at Maubeuge was threatened in three ways by a devil who tripped her in such a manner that she fell into a fire while trying to hang up a pot of boiling water. Happily she was wearing the cast-off habit of abbess Aldegund and it protected her from all injury.66 Two nuns at Laon were both assigned to do the washing and came into potential conflict because one of them had used all the pothooks to lower her cauldron over the inadequate fire.The spirit of community prevailed and she agreed to share her equipment but both despaired of lack of fuel to build up the fire.When they prayed together, however, a thunderclap was heard. The beam which held the pot bent down to the fire, which, fueled supernaturally, flickered up and boiled their water.67

These stories, preserved and ritually repeated in Merovingian convents, give shape and meaning to a dreary and often unwelcome round of daily life for the women who willingly or unwillingly struggled to live together in harmony.They illustrate the petty temptations against which the spirit of charity had to strive.At last, there was something grander at the core to lift their spirits to the ultimate goal of their consecrated life.The regular offices of psalm singing and praying in unison dominated the life of the sisterhoods. The rules they lived by outlined the round of chant adapted to the changing seasons and penalised any sister who put personal inconvenience or necessity above the chain of prayer which bound them together.The importance of this communal response was vividly invoked by the life of Austreberta.The abbess was awakened by a sister who had been told in a vision to gather the community instantly for prayer. She struck the signal and instantly the well-trained sisterhood rose and hastened to chapel.Just as they disposed themselves for their office, the roof under which they had been sleeping collapsed. Of the whole monastery, only the chapel and one window arch sheltering two infants who had been left to sleep remained.The only sister who was injured was one who went out of the chapel against the abbess’ orders to see what had happened.Happily,the mortal injuries inflicted by falling masonry were cured by the miraculous touch of the saint.68

The most terrible punishment the rules could inflict was excommunication—not from the sacraments of the church, which did not lie under the jurisdiction of the abbess or her nuns—but from the choir where they worshipped together. A sister who exhibited irreparable pride or made repeated breeches of humility and obedience could be punished with virtual exclusion from the community. She would have to stand apart, singing in a different mode and conducting her services alone, deprived of her place in the sisterhood until she had gained their pardon and reinstatement. She might even lose her seniority in the ranks and be reduced to a place at the end of the line.This was the final ordeal in a community so geared to sharing and the suppression of individuality.The triumph of the ideal was the creation of the choir—the earthly sisterhood who stand at the deathbed of every nun, chanting in unison to drown out their own tears and help their comrade into the place awaiting her in the heavenly choir.

When Saint Anstrude was told of her brother’s murder, she fell into an inconsolable paroxysm of grief. She wasrestored to herself only when her sisters, in pairs and groups came to her in the oratory, wept with her, shared her prayers and gradually folded her back into her place among them, reminding her that when one member suffers the whole body is in pain.69 When her brother’s murderers invaded her sanctuary, threatening her life, they were confronted with an advancing cohort of nuns singing together in defence of their mother. A globe of heavenly fire rose from the towers of their chapel and the invaders fled in fear.70

This was the end of their ordeal: a sisterhood that mirrored on earth the angelic choirs, the gliding stream of virgins singing a song which only they could hear before the throne of God. The rules written by bishops and monks frame the limits of their aspirations. The lives mirrored in the records of their saints and their sisters point to their unlimited fulfilment.
