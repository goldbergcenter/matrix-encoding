2 Encrateia is a Greek word meaning abstinence from sexual activity. It gave the name to an early Christian sect called Encratites who claimed that sexual purity was a necessity for salvation. I have adopted it to avoid the clumsy use of “chaste celibacy” to describe sexually inactive unmarried people because it encourages the misuse of celibacy, a key concept in most ages of church reform.

3 Following the modern trend in monastic studies, I have abandoned the term “double monasteries” as being too formal to describe the many variations that appear in the texts. Small communities of men were generally attached to women’s houses in a subordinate capacity. Men’s communities generally had informal communities of women attached to them, sometimes as subordinates and sometimes as independent religious persons seeking protection, the sort of arrangement Jutta of Sponheim had with the monks of Disibodenberg. Other communities were founded by male and female members of the same family and the two sexes lived together on equal terms.

9 I have explored this milieu for the tenth and eleventh centuries in some detail in Chapter 7 of Sisters in Arms: Catholic Nuns Through Two Millennia (Cambridge MA: Harvard University Press, 1996). The dimensions of nuns’ involvement in ecclesiastical and worldly affairs in the twelfth century may be measured by the continuing authority of the Spanish abbess of Las Huelgas de Burgos who was criticised by Innocent III in his bull Nova Quaedam. Exempt from episcopal authority, she had the right to confer benefices on clergy and invest them with the care of souls, to establish new parishes and discipline priests suspected of heresy. Presiding like a bishop over her courts, the abbess also promulgated decrees and indulgences from Rome and Sancha Garcia (1207–30) claimed she could hear confessions.
