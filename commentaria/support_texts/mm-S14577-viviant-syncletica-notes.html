3 Regnault, 37. The organisation of Chapter 3 of Regnault’s study has unintended implications. Regnault follows his discussion of the lives of several eminent female monastics with sections on “Illusions diaboliques” and “La sexualité au désert.” There is no doubt that demonic temptation and sexuality are important topics, and there is no doubt that many male monks saw women as sexual temptresses. 18 On her name, see Henry George Liddell and Robert Scott, eds., A Greek-English Lexicon (Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1968) 1665B, s.v. sugklhtikovı. Sunkletikos refers to a man of senatorial rank (e.g., Chronicon Paschale: PG 92:752A), while the feminine adjective sunkletike refers to a woman of senatorial rank or the wife of a senator. See, for example, R. Cagnet, et al., eds., Inscriptiones graecæ ad res romanas pertinentes, vol. 3 (Paris, 1906) 3.95 (pp. 33-34): ajdelfo;n de; th¿ı krativsthı sunklhtikh¿ı Klaudivaı Pauvlh”. Given the title of recension b, “Life of a Certain Holy Virgin,” and the fact that Syncletica is not named in the body of any of the manuscripts, it is probable that her name was a later addition. Originally anonymous, she was probably given her name because of her position in society.

There was a famous Syncletica in fourth-century Egypt, considered worthy enough to have her biography attributed to St. Athanasius, author of the Life of Anthony. See Pseudo-Athanasius, Vita et gesta sanctae beataeque magistrae Syncleticae (PG 28: 1487-1558). For a translation with an introduction and notes, see Elizabeth Castelli, “Pseudo-Athanasius: The Life and Activity of the Holy and Blessed Teacher Syncletica,” Asceticism in Greco-Roman Antiquity: A Sourcebook, ed. Vincent L. Wimbush (Minneapolis: Fortress Press, 1990) 265-311. Another translation, by Elizabeth Bongie, is planned for the Peregrina Translations Series, Peregrina Publishing Co. For a French translation, see Sr. Odile Bénédite Bernard, Vie de Sainte Syncletique, Spiritualité Orientale 9; (Abbaye Notre Dame de Bellefontaine, 1972).

The ancient author of the Life of Syncletica of Egypt derives her name from “the heavenly assembly” (sunkleµtos); the name would then suggest “she who is called to the assembly” (Castelli, “Pseudo-Athanasius,” 267).  But sunkleµtos can also mean “senate”; see Liddell and Scott, 1665B, s.v. sugklhtikovı, II.2. Castelli notes, 267 n. 7, that in the case of Syncletica of Egypt, both the name and the person may be an invention.
