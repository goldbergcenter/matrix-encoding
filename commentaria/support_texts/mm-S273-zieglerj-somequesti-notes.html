13Two commentaries on papers of mine were especially revealing concerning the current confusion over the precise nature of the Belgian beguinal movement. The first was by Professor Colin Eisler (New York University, The Institute of Fine Arts) at the symposium on Radical Physicality, Worcester, MA, November 1985. 14 Since the publication of Herbert Grundmann’s Religiöse Bewegungen im Mittelalter (2nd ed. Hildesheim: Georg Olms, 1961) and his many articles during the 1960’s, most beguine research has been published as a detail of the larger picture of heresy and the Inquisition. See, for example, the studies by Bolton, Lambert, Lerner, Little, Schmitt, etc. Heresy and the Inquisition were especially active in southern France and the cities along the Rhine and, relative to these areas, the largest quantity of documents were produced here. Hence to view the Belgian beguines as part of the history of the Rhine and southern France yields a less than accurate picture unless one understands fully the distinct part the Belgian beguines played in it as isolated from the Rhenish movement and as having provided the standard whereby the “good” beguines east of the Rhine might be judged.

20 Any student of beguine history would be wise to read work for its geographical and chronological specificity. Belgium was a distinct geo-political entity during the Middle Ages. Before World War I, it was recognised by German and Belgian scholars alike that Belgium was the old western territory of the German Empire, a separate political territory but one which owed service to the Empire. For reasons far too complex to enter here, having mostly to do with the increased interest in world-historical events after the first World War, Belgium’s mediaeval history (its separateness) became the charge of “locals” rather than internationals. Philippen’s work thus fell into the former category.
